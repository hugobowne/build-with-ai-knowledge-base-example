The most common mistake in AI development is trying to improve a system without first understanding how it fails. Hamel Hussein's insight, cited throughout the course, is deceptively simple: improvement requires observation. Yet teams routinely skip the observation step, jumping straight from "it doesn't work well enough" to "let me change the prompt." The data flywheel -- the self-reinforcing cycle where evaluation outputs become development inputs -- is what separates teams that escape [[proof_of_concept_purgatory]] from those that spin their wheels indefinitely.

The flywheel starts with looking at your data. Eddie Wharton calls this the highest-impact activity most teams neglect: "Standing up evals without actually looking at your data... hypothesizing what matters without actually looking at the interactions that users are having with your system." The "vibes" stage of [[evaluation_driven_development]] is not a pejorative. Manual review of outputs -- studying them in spreadsheets, building intuition about where the system succeeds and fails -- is where evaluation begins. Hugo demonstrates this concretely by annotating 25 input pairs, yielding 50 individual rows, achieving 92% accuracy against ground truth. That sounds respectable until confusion matrix analysis reveals a systematic pattern: his judgments favored broad, contextual summaries while the ground truth favored specific but cryptic ones. This error analysis -- the transition from "I got 92%" to "I systematically overcredit breadth" -- is the moment where the flywheel begins to turn.

The fuel for the flywheel is [[annotation_and_data_labeling]], and its quality depends on decisions made before the first label is applied. Ines Montani's principle -- factor business logic out of classification logic -- reshapes how annotation tasks should be designed. Do not teach a model to recognize "minor date of birth" when the concept changes daily; separate what the model classifies from what code computes. The S&P Global case study quantifies the impact: breaking multi-label annotation into single-label passes was 10x faster overall, with production models requiring just 15 hours of single-annotator time. The lesson generalizes: annotation design decisions have outsized downstream impact, and reducing cognitive load per annotation pass dramatically increases both speed and consistency.

[[non_determinism]] -- the "double entropy problem" of variance in both inputs and outputs -- is what makes the flywheel necessary in the first place and what makes it technically challenging. You cannot characterize an LLM system with a spot check. Natalia Rodnova suggests Monte Carlo-style repeated runs -- executing the same test multiple times and analyzing the distribution of outputs rather than checking for exact matches. Stefan Krawczyk extends this with PyTest Harvest, which collects results across multiple dimensions simultaneously. The shift from "assert output equals expected" to "characterize the output distribution" is a fundamental change in how quality is understood. It is also what connects evaluation to [[observability_and_tracing]]: you cannot measure a distribution you cannot observe, and the context-dependent nature of LLM calls means that what you sent to the model -- not just what came back -- determines whether the system worked correctly.

As the flywheel accelerates, automation becomes both possible and necessary. The [[llm_as_judge]] pattern scales human-calibrated evaluation to production volumes. The design hierarchy is clear: code-based checks first (regex, structured output validation, tool-call verification), because they are cheaper and more reliable. LLM judges only for subjective, contextual criteria -- tone, empathy, clinical accuracy, whether alien dialogue sounds sufficiently extraterrestrial. But the critical insight, from William Horton's production experience at Maven Clinic, is that LLM judges must be anchored to human judgment. He uses two human labelers to establish inter-annotator agreement, then calibrates the judge against that baseline. The 66% unanimous agreement ceiling from a previous 12-class intent classification project is perhaps the most important number in this wiki: it means that even humans cannot agree reliably on many evaluation tasks, and that ceiling limits what any automated system can achieve. Eddie Wharton's CJ package formalizes this calibration with causal inference techniques, but most teams are not even doing error analysis properly yet. The maturity arc is long, and each team must walk it.

William Horton's paired classifier pattern adds another layer of sophistication: before the judge evaluates a response, a classifier determines whether the judge should even apply. Evaluating clinical accuracy on "how do I cancel my appointment?" produces noise, not signal. This routing step -- matching evaluation criteria to conversation types -- is essential for producing actionable metrics in production systems with heterogeneous query distributions.

[[synthetic_data_and_simulation]] injects energy into the flywheel at multiple points. Zach Muller's distillation example -- 14,000 training examples generated for $5 from a 30B parameter model -- demonstrates the economics of using large models to bootstrap training data for smaller ones. Hugo's synthetic query generation for retrieval evaluation addresses the chicken-and-egg problem of needing evaluation data to improve a system that you need to improve before you can generate good evaluation data. But synthetic data has limits that William Horton's experience makes vivid: multi-turn conversation simulation requires a third "moderator" component because neither the agent LLM nor the simulated user LLM wants to stop talking. Keeping simulated users in character across turns is surprisingly difficult. Synthetic data gets the system to a testable state; real user data reveals what actually matters.

The flywheel's output is not just better models but better understanding. Annotation disagreements surface organizational misalignment -- when co-founders disagree on labels, Stella Liu observes, the issue is in the organization, not the evaluation system. Failure analysis reveals systematic biases in how the team thinks about quality. Production evaluation with real users shows what people actually need, often diverging from what the team predicted. Each turn of the flywheel generates artifacts -- labeled data, error taxonomies, evaluation criteria, calibrated judges -- that become inputs for the next turn.

Ines Montani captures the long game of this flywheel: use LLMs to build the system rather than being the system. Model-assisted annotation, where even a 50% accurate model pre-annotating data saves 50% of human effort, accelerates the creation of training data for smaller, faster, cheaper models that can eventually replace expensive API calls. The S&P Global pipeline -- LLM-assisted annotation producing tiny 6MB production models -- is the flywheel's ultimate destination for teams that can invest in the full cycle. For others, the flywheel operates within the [[evaluation_driven_development]] loop: observe, annotate, evaluate, iterate, observe again. The data gets better. The understanding deepens. The system improves. But only if someone, at the beginning, sits down and looks at the data.
