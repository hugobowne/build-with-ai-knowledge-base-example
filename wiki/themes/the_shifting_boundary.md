In 2023, making an LLM follow a multi-step plan required extensive scaffolding -- explicit chain-of-thought prompts, step-tracking harnesses, retry logic for when the model lost its thread. By 2025, frontier models plan natively, manage multi-step reasoning internally, and adjust their own effort levels per query. The capabilities that developers built around models keep migrating into the models themselves, and the boundary between what the model does and what the developer builds is shifting faster than any other aspect of the field.

Sebastian Raschka's deep dive in [[llm_architecture_rasbt]] explains the mechanism: inference scaling. Rather than investing compute solely in larger pre-training runs, modern models invest compute at inference time through longer reasoning chains, parallel sampling of candidate answers, sequential refinement, and external judges that verify intermediate steps. RLVR -- reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards -- trains models on agent traces, teaching them the behaviors that harnesses previously enforced: planning before acting, breaking tasks into sub-steps, checking work. The practical result is that each new model release makes some piece of surrounding infrastructure obsolete.

Doug Turnbull's Kirby analogy, cited by Hugo, captures this dynamic vividly: models absorb the [[agent_harnesses]] around them, like Kirby from Nintendo absorbing enemies' powers. Labs report ripping out their harnesses with each new model release. Nicholas Moy, who built the first multi-hop agent at Windsurf, advises builders to "rip out the harness, let your model be free." The Gemini Interactions API, discussed as a future abstraction, represents providers absorbing harness functionality into their APIs directly.

Yet the picture is more nuanced than "models will do everything." The course reveals a layered reality. At the capability frontier -- coding, reasoning, planning -- models are indeed absorbing what developers used to build. Hugo demonstrates a coding agent in 131 lines of Python with four tools that can organize files, write scripts, and hot-reload new capabilities at runtime. No system prompt is needed for basic operation because models have gotten good enough at interpreting tool descriptions alone. Ivan Leo's ~1,500-line deep research agent is "actually quite not that much code" for the capabilities it provides. The minimalism is the point: as models absorb more, the surrounding code gets thinner.

But the code that remains is not trivial -- it is the code that models cannot replace. [[context_engineering]] is a prime example. LLMs are stateless. Every piece of information a model "knows" during a conversation had to be placed in its context window by something external. Lance Martin's four patterns -- write, select, compress, isolate -- are developer-built infrastructure that models need but cannot provide for themselves. Even as context windows grow to one million tokens (Ravin Kumar describes Notebook LM simplifying its retrieval as Gemini's window expanded), the engineering of what goes into that window remains a developer responsibility. The model cannot decide what it does not know. Someone -- or some system -- has to bring the right information at the right time.

The "skills" pattern makes this concrete. Skills are markdown files that encode procedural and informational knowledge, disclosed progressively as the agent encounters tasks that require them. Stefan Krawczyk calls this "smart context engineering" and notes that "people two years ago doing really fine-tuned context engineering would see this is an obvious pattern." The pattern predates the terminology, but the underlying work -- deciding what information the model needs and when -- does not go away as models improve. It changes form.

[[model_selection_and_tradeoffs]] sits at the center of the shifting boundary. The Pareto frontier runs from tiny models (Gemma 270M on a Raspberry Pi) through Flash-tier workhorses to frontier reasoning models. Ivan Leo's advice -- "spend today for the models of tomorrow" -- captures the temporal dimension: capabilities that are expensive today will be cheap tomorrow. But William Horton's pragmatic production experience pushes back: Gemini Flash is his default despite its limitations because latency matters today, not tomorrow. The strategy of smart models for planning and cheap models for execution is itself a response to the shifting boundary -- the lead agent uses more intelligence because routing decisions are high-leverage, while sub-agents use cheaper models because their tasks are constrained.

[[fine_tuning_and_distillation]] represents developers pushing back against the boundary from the other direction. Rather than waiting for models to absorb a capability, distillation creates small specialized models that do specific tasks cheaply. Zach Muller's 14,000 examples for $5, distilled into a tiny Qwen model for natural language to bash translation, demonstrates the economics. Ines Montani's philosophy -- use LLMs to build smaller, faster systems rather than being the production system -- is a deliberate strategy of creating lightweight models that occupy the developer-built side of the boundary permanently. The S&P Global pipeline produces 6MB models trained on 15 hours of annotation. These models are not trying to compete with frontier systems; they are doing a specific job cheaply and reliably, forever.

[[vibe_coding_and_ai_assisted_development]] is where the shifting boundary touches every developer personally. The spectrum from pure vibe coding (generating code without understanding) to structured AI-assisted development (specs, tests, architectural control) is a microcosm of the broader question: as models get more capable, how much understanding must the developer maintain? Eric Ma's cautionary tale -- "opusmaxing" Canvas Chat into 8,000+ line monoliths that became impossible to extend -- shows what happens when the boundary shifts and the developer does not adapt. Carol Willing's counterpoint -- that abstraction layers are fundamental to computing history, and AI-generated code may be the next layer -- frames the optimistic case. The resolution, from Eric Ma's practice, is that architectural awareness is the irreducible skill: you can let the model write the code, but you must maintain control over the structure. Specs and tests serve as double constraints, preventing the coding agent from going off the rails.

[[prompt_engineering]] itself illustrates the shifting boundary. Hugo nearly removed chain-of-thought from his 10 principles of effective prompting, given that reasoning models have adjustable effort built in. But Caleb Tutty pushed back: explicit step-by-step instructions still produce "quite dramatically" different results even with high-effort reasoning enabled. The boundary between prompting technique and trained behavior is blurry and model-specific. Drew Brunig's research shows coding harnesses with thousands of tokens "fighting the weights" -- system prompts trying to override deeply trained behaviors. William Horton's experience at Maven, writing regex to strip Gemini's persistent medical disclaimers because no system prompt could override them, is the most vivid example: sometimes the boundary is a wall.

The forward-looking prediction from [[ws_7_workflows_multiagent_and_context_engineering]] is that all agents will move toward higher context and action complexity, but models and APIs will absorb that complexity. If true, the distinction between agents and workflows may eventually dissolve as the infrastructure handles what developers currently manage manually. But this prediction has been directionally correct and temporally wrong before -- the future arrives unevenly. Hugo's practical advice is to build for the capabilities that exist, architect for the ones coming, and keep the API call as your anchor point. The boundary will keep shifting. What stays constant is the need to understand where it is right now.
