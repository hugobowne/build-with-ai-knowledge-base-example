The demo works beautifully. A five-line quickstart produces an answer that makes the room gasp. A stakeholder leans forward. Someone says the word "transformative." And then, slowly, everything falls apart.

This is the story of [[proof_of_concept_purgatory]] -- the central problem that motivates the entire body of knowledge in this wiki. Hugo Bowne-Anderson identifies the core dynamic in [[ws_1_foundations]]: AI flips the traditional software excitement curve. In conventional software, you struggle first and get rewarded later. In AI, you get the wow moment first -- the flashy demo, the viral tweet -- then fall into difficulty as hallucinations surface, monitoring gaps compound, and integration issues multiply. Brad Morris captures the experience bluntly: "the idea and what you see on Twitter doesn't really line up."

The path from demo to production is not a straight line but a disciplined cycle, and it begins with understanding what you are actually building. The [[five_first_principles]] established in Workshop 1 -- API calls as the atomic unit, non-determinism as a fundamental property, logging from day zero, evaluation as the backbone, and rapid iteration -- are specifically designed as an escape route. They interact as a system: understanding the API call anatomy lets you observe non-determinism in practice; logging captures that behavior; evaluation measures it; iteration improves it. Hugo calls AI systems "organic," requiring gardener-like attention rather than set-and-forget deployment. The five principles are the gardener's essential tools.

The first trap teams encounter on this journey is premature abstraction. Hugo demonstrates in [[ws_1_foundations]] how Llama Index's quickstart hides the model, the prompt, and all retrieval internals -- prompting Hamel Hussein's reaction: "F You, Show Me the Prompt." This connects directly to the [[framework_selection]] debate that becomes one of the course's most heated exchanges. Stefan Krawczyk offers a diagnostic: does a framework optimize for 0-to-1 (getting a prototype running) or 1-to-N (maintaining a production system)? Tools that obscure the [[the_api_call_mental_model]] may accelerate the demo but trap you in purgatory. William Horton's position is even sharper -- he wants the raw SDK with a LiteLLM gateway, nothing more. The reconciliation principle is to control the interface and hide the implementation, allowing framework swaps without rewriting the system.

Escaping purgatory requires [[observability_and_tracing]] from the first day of development -- not as an afterthought bolted on before launch. Stefan makes the case forcefully: "the only way that you can debug what happened is dependent on what you observe, because what you send to the LLM is context-dependent." This is a genuine departure from traditional software engineering, where observability is primarily a production concern. In AI systems, the non-deterministic nature of outputs means you need trace visibility during development itself. Hugo starts with SQLite and Datasette -- lightweight, immediate, sufficient. As systems mature, OpenTelemetry becomes the standard, with the firm advice to avoid tools not compatible with it.

With observability in place, [[evaluation_driven_development]] becomes possible. The maturity arc runs from manual "vibes" -- looking at outputs in spreadsheets, building intuition -- through systematic failure analysis to automated eval harnesses. The critical insight is that most teams skip the middle step. They go straight from eyeballing outputs to trying to automate evaluation, missing the failure analysis that reveals why the system breaks, not just how often. Hugo's own annotation exercise demonstrates this: 92% accuracy sounds good until confusion matrix analysis reveals a systematic bias toward breadth over specificity. That pattern is invisible without structured failure analysis and drives the creation of competing prompts that can be tested head-to-head.

The transition from development to production introduces new physics. Stefan's two-loop model -- an inner development loop (local testing, prompt tuning) and an outer production loop (deployed application, user telemetry feeding back) -- provides the operational framework. These loops run continuously in parallel, not sequentially. William Horton's Maven Clinic launch illustrates the outer loop's revelatory power: within 24 hours, real users showed that they frequently wanted to update profile data -- a mundane, high-volume need the team had never prioritized. "Until you get the real user data, you don't actually know what people want it to do."

The [[production_ai_patterns]] that emerge from these case studies are concrete and recurring. Progressive rollout -- from team to company to 20% of external users -- is not just risk management but discovery, with each expansion surfacing new input distributions. Provider fallbacks are essential infrastructure, not optional; Stefan's veterinary transcription system learned this the hard way when OpenAI went down. Cost management operates at every level, from model selection (Gemini Flash for latency, GPT-5.4 Mini for accuracy) to infrastructure (Modal's pay-for-compute economics) to the cost of observability itself.

The full [[ai_sdlc]] -- build, deploy, monitor, evaluate, iterate -- is deceptively simple to state and genuinely demanding to execute. But the course demonstrates, through William Horton's multi-month progression at Maven, through Stefan's 60-call veterinary transcription DAG, through student projects at demo day, that the path out of purgatory is walkable. It requires not a single breakthrough but the disciplined application of practices that, individually, are each surprisingly straightforward. The hard part is not any one principle. The hard part is doing all of them, continuously, from the start.
